1. Music Notation¶

Music is usually notated on music staffs. The position of a note within the system indicates it's unique note name, while the appearance of the given note is used to denote the rhythmic value (relative duration) assigned to a given note. Notes are named after the first 7 letters of the alphabet. As you can see on the stylized piano keyboard above, notes can be altered by means of using accidentals. If we take one note and alter it one semitone to the right (from a white key to it's neighbouring black key, if there is one), we add a 'sharp' (#) to its note name. If we lower a note by the same amount, we add a 'flat' (b).

Two notes that in the above representation fall on the same piano key, yet are arrived at by altering different original notes, are sometimes referred to as enharmonically identical. This way of thinking is only possible while within the framework of equal temperament.

The distance from a given note to the next note in either direction on the keyboard that shares its name as a letter is referred to as an octave. The octave is the first type of musical interval that you will need to remember. It spans the distance of 12 semi-tones and it's frequency ratio is 1/2.

For all of you music21 lovers out there, please note this easier way of entering notes using a function called tinyNotation.

Furthermore, every piece is usually rhythmically specified by means of a time signature. 2. Musical Intervals¶

Musical intervals are relevant to both harmonic and melodic description of musical phenomena. (naive / practical definition: melody being the successive sounding of musical pitches rather than their simultaneous performance)

A definition of melodic intervals that is sufficient to the enterprise of this notebook is: musical intervals are a measure of melodic distance between distinct note events. They are categorized based on the distance between two notes, which is in turn measured in semitones. As remains to be shown below, they also form the basis for classifying chordal (or: harmonic) structures in music.

When calculating pitch classes for a given interval, keep in mind that there is no black keys between B/C and E/F on the piano, i.e. that when altering the pitch class "B" with a single sharp accidental, the resulting note will be enharmonically identical to a simple "C".

The following table lists melodic intervals within the span of one octave (12 semitones) with their associated distance in semitones:

number of semitones corresponding melodic interval(s) example
1 minor second C - Db
2 major second C - D
3 minor third C - Eb
4 major third C - E
5 perfect fourth C - F
6 augmented fourth, tritone, diminished fifth C - F#, C - Gb
7 perfect fifth C - G
8 minor sixth C - Ab
9 major sixth C - A
10 minor seventh C - Bb
11 major seventh C - B
12 octave C - C

In general, it is advisable that you learn the interval labels and their corresponding number of semitones by heart. Let us look at these intervals in actual music notation.

3. Chords / Harmonies¶

Going up a level in size, the next relevant buiding block to describing tonal music are chords. For our purposes, a chord can be defined as a structure consisting of three or four distinct, simultaneously sounded notes. Therefore, so far, notes (pitch classes) are our most basic element. They make up intervals, which can in turn be combined into chords.

The most basic form of chord we will be encountering consists of three different pitch classes and is referred to as a triad:

In [11]:
C = chord.Chord(['C4', 'E4', 'G4'])
C.addLyric('C / "C major"')
C.show()

The above example already shows you a way that chords are usually labelled. In music, chords are generally categorized based on their interval structure. Triads in their most basic appearance are merely two thirds stacked on top of a given root note. Depending on which kinds of triads are stacked, the respective chords are categorized as follows:

interval structure chord label (e.g., root "C") chord class name example pitch classes
major third - minor third C major C - E - G
minor third - major third Cm minor C - Eb - G
major third - major third C aug / $C^{+}$ augmented C - E - G#
minor third - minor third C dim / $C^{0}$ diminished C - Eb - Gb
major second - perfect fourth Csus4 suspended 4th / sus4 C - F - G
perfect fourth - major second Csus2 suspended 2nd / sus2 C - D - G

Let us look at these examples in regular music notation:

In [12]:
chords = converter.parse('tinyNotation: chord{c1 e g}, chord{c1 e- g}, chord{c1 e g#}, chord{c1 e- g-}, chord{c1 f g}, chord{c1 d g}')
chordtypes = ["major", "minor", "augmented", "diminished", "sus4", "sus2"]

for a in range(len(chords)):
    for i in chords[a].getElementsByClass(chord.Chord):
        i.addLyric(chordtypes[a])
chords.show()

4. Scales¶

Another building block of tonal music is usually referred to as scales. In music, scales can be considered both as a harmonic reservoire, as well as a melodic constituent.

There is literature (e.g., Haunschild 1994) suggesting that scales are the fundamental entity that harmony, melody, as well as composition and improvisation draw upon.

Be that as it may - the (to us) most common scale - is called a major scale. It consists of a collection of seven different pitch classes that constitute its tonal material. Spelled out in an ascending melody and repeating the root note an octave above, it looks as follows:

In [31]:
cscale = converter.parse("tinyNotation: c2, d2, e2, f2, g2, a2, b2, c'2 ")

for a in range(len(cscale)):
    for i in cscale[a].getElementsByClass(note.Note):
        i.addLyric(i.nameWithOctave)
cscale.show()

There is a lot more different scales than just this major scale. You have encountered one of them already (the so-called "lydian scale"). However, there is a few characteristics that make scales more interesting when describing tonality:

  • a certain scale is categorized by the succession of semitones and whole tones it contains.
  • a major scale is composed as follows: root - whole - whole - half - whole - whole - whole - half (the last interval being the distance to the repetition of the first (root) note an octave above.
  • If you stack thirds on every single note of a given scale and do not add any accidentals that the scale did not have before, you get chords that are very closely related to the scale at hand:
In [34]:
csc = converter.parse("tinyNotation: chord{c2 e g}, chord{d2 f a}, chord{e2 g b}, chord{f2 a c'}, chord{g2 b d'}, chord{a2 c' e'}, chord{b2 d' f'}, chord{c'2 e' g'}")

cclasses1 = ["C", "Dm", "Em", "F", "G", "Am", "B dim", "C"]
cclasses2 = ["I major", "ii minor", "iii minor", "IV major", "V major", "vi minor", "vii diminished", "I major"]
ct = 0
for a in range(len(csc)):
    for i in csc[a].getElementsByClass(chord.Chord):
        i.addLyric(cclasses1[ct])
        i.addLyric(cclasses2[ct])
        ct += 1
        
csc.show()

This last bit of information is very relevant for musical analysis, as it introduces the notion of labelling chords by mean of roman numerals. We will get back to this a little later this semester.

As you have seen, this means that scales are not just a succession of notes. From this perspective, they also seem to imply certain chords that befit them.

Please remember that a major scale is defined as eight successive notes (seven distinct pitch classes) and that the semitone steps are always located between the third/fourth and seventh note/octave of a given scale. If that does not align with the chosen root, we will have to use accidentals. For example, when constructing a G major scale, without accidentals, the second semitone would be found between the sixth and seventh step of the scale (E-F). Therefore, we have to add an accidental to the seventh note in order to make the scale construction work. The resulting pitch classes for G major would be:

G - A - B - C - D - E - F# - G
